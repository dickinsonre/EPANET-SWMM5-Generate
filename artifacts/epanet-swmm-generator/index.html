EPANET 2.3 & SWMM 5.2.4 Network Generator

Single-file HTML application • ~160 KB • No dependencies • Mobile-optimized

Overview

This application generates hydraulically valid, calibration-ready water distribution (EPANET) and stormwater collection (SWMM) models of any size — from 50-node teaching examples to 100,000-node city-scale networks. Each model includes a procedurally generated city map backdrop with streets, buildings, parks, lakes, rivers, railroads, and highways. Models are exportable as .inp files that load directly into EPANET 2.2+, EPA SWMM 5.2+, WNTR, PySWMM, InfoWorks ICM, or any compatible hydraulic modeling platform.

EPANET 2.3 Mode — Water Distribution

Network Topology: Grid-based layout with configurable jitter (position perturbation), diagonal cross-connections for looping, and dead-end branches. Grid size from 3×3 to 316×316. Eight presets from Tiny (50 nodes) through City (100K nodes).

Terrain: Five terrain models — rolling hills with NW→SE slope, flat, central valley, ridge line, and random Perlin-like noise. Configurable elevation range (default 58–202 ft) with multiple hill features.

Reservoirs: Placed at perimeter nodes and fully integrated into the grid — the reservoir replaces the junction node, inheriting all pipe connections. No floating nodes. Source pumps are only created when the reservoir head is insufficient for gravity feed.

Pump Curves: Sized via BFS critical-path analysis. The engine traces from each reservoir through the pipe network using Hazen-Williams friction loss to find the farthest node, then computes the required pump head as elevation deficit + computed friction + safety margin. Three-point curves bracket the operating point (shutoff = 150% design head, max flow = 400% design flow or design+500 GPM whichever is larger) to prevent "exceeds maximum flow" warnings. Booster pumps are sized based on actual pipe diameter and downstream elevation needs.

Tanks: Placed in the distribution field. Base elevation computed to serve the local pressure zone at ~85% of source HGL. Tank nodes replace junction nodes, same as reservoirs.

Pipes: Length = Euclidean distance × 1.3 street-routing factor. Diameters: transmission (24–36") along corridors and near sources, secondary (12–20") on arterials every 5th row/column, distribution (6–12") elsewhere. C-factors spatially correlated: downtown (center) = old pipes (C=80–100), suburban (edges) = new pipes (C=110–130).

Pipe Age: Each pipe assigned an installation year correlated with distance from center (1950s downtown → 2020s suburban). Written as decade-tagged link attributes (YR1960s, YR1990s, etc.).

Pumps & Valves: No parallel links. Every pump and valve is inserted by splitting an existing pipe and creating an intermediate junction. Booster pumps include a closed bypass pipe for isolation. PRVs are placed at elevation zone boundaries (pressure zones auto-detected when terrain range exceeds 80 ft).

Pressure Zones: When the elevation range exceeds 80 ft, the system is divided into zones of ~70 ft each. PRVs are placed at pipes that cross zone boundaries, with settings computed from downstream elevation + 50 psi target.

Demand Zones: Four zones spatially clustered (not random): Industrial seeded in NW corner, Commercial along a central spine, Mixed in a defined area, Residential fills everywhere else. Four diurnal patterns with realistic multipliers (residential morning/evening peaks, commercial midday, industrial plateau, mixed blend).

Calibration Features: Emitter nodes (leaks/hydrants), optional chlorine water quality model with bulk/wall decay, secondary demands for unaccounted-for water and fire flow, node/pipe tags for zone-based grouping.

Connectivity Validation: Post-generation BFS from all sources verifies every junction is reachable. Isolated node count displayed in Stats.

Supply-Demand Check: Total average and peak demand (×1.6 peaking factor) are computed and compared to supply capacity. Green/red indicator in Stats tab.

SWMM 5.2.4 Mode — Stormwater & Sanitary Sewer

Junctions: Manholes with invert elevation = ground elevation − cover depth. Max depth computed from ground-to-invert difference. Spatially clustered land-use zones control impervious percentage.

Subcatchments: Drainage areas delineated using D8 flow direction routing on the DEM grid. Each cell flows to its steepest downhill neighbor until reaching a local pit or grid edge; all cells draining to the same terminus are aggregated into a subcatchment. Area, width, slope, and percent impervious vary by land-use zone.

Conduits: Gravity sewers oriented from higher to lower invert elevation. 10 conduit cross-section shapes: Circular, Rectangular Closed, Arch, Egg, Horseshoe, Gothic, Catenary, Basket Handle, Semi-Elliptical, Semi-Circular — with configurable mix percentages. Diameters grade from collectors (12–24") at periphery to trunk sewers (36–60") at center.

Culverts: Configurable count with entrance loss coefficients (Kentry=0.5, Kexit=1.0) and FHWA culvert codes (circular concrete, CMP, box concrete, box headwall).

Outfalls: Placed at the lowest-elevation perimeter nodes. FREE discharge type.

Design Storms: SCS Type I, SCS Type II (24-hr), Uniform (6-hr), and Chicago (2-hr) design storms with configurable total depth. Written as TIMESERIES at 30-min or 5-min intervals.

Infiltration: Three methods — Horton (with configurable max/min rates and decay constant), Green-Ampt (suction, Ksat, IMD), and Curve Number. Parameters vary by subcatchment.

Water Quality: Up to 8 pollutants (TSS, BOD, COD, TN, TP, Zinc, Oil & Grease, E.coli). EMC washoff with zone-specific concentrations. Four land uses (Residential, Commercial, Industrial, OpenSpace) with coverage assignments. Full POLLUTANTS, LANDUSES, COVERAGES, BUILDUP, and WASHOFF sections.

Groundwater: Aquifer definition with configurable porosity, field capacity, Ksat, and groundwater table elevation. Per-subcatchment GROUNDWATER entries with A1/B1 lateral flow coefficients.

RDII (Rainfall-Derived Inflow & Infiltration): Five spatially-distributed RTK unit hydrograph groups (UH1–UH5). Old downtown sewers get UH1 (high R values = more I&I), newer suburban sewers get UH5 (low R). Each group has randomized R/T/K for short, medium, and long triangle responses. Configurable % of nodes receiving RDII and sewer area per node.

Dry Weather Flow: Three zone-specific DWF patterns (Residential diurnal with morning/evening peaks, Commercial midday-heavy, Industrial sustained plateau). Optional weekend and monthly seasonal patterns. Configurable % of nodes with DWF and base flow magnitude.

Direct Inflows: Configurable number of nodes with triangle-hydrograph inflow timeseries. Staggered start times, configurable peak flow, ramp-up time, and duration. Optional TSS concentration timeseries with first-flush exponential decay.

LID Controls: Seven types — Bio-Retention Cell, Permeable Pavement, Infiltration Trench, Rain Barrel, Vegetative Swale, Green Roof, Rain Garden. Full multi-layer definitions (SURFACE, SOIL, STORAGE, PAVEMENT, DRAIN, DRAINMAT). Configurable % of subcatchments receiving LID.

Snowmelt: Optional. Dividing temperature, melt coefficient, ATI weight. Snowpack definitions for plowable, impervious, and pervious surfaces. Temperature timeseries with diurnal variation.

Unit Systems

US Customary: EPANET uses GPM, ft, in. SWMM uses CFS, ft, in, acres.

SI / Metric: EPANET uses LPS, m, mm. SWMM uses CMS, m, mm, hectares. All input fields update labels when switching units. The generator converts internally and writes the correct FLOW_UNITS in OPTIONS.

City Map Features

Procedurally generated backdrop with three visual styles (Light paper map, Satellite dark, Blueprint). Features include: arterial and local street grids with organic wobble, building footprints with rotation, irregularly-shaped parks with tree dots, organic lake/pond shapes, sinuous river with bank shading, dashed railroad tracks, highway with center line markings, named districts, scale bar, and north arrow.

Export Formats

.inp file: EPANET 2.2+ or SWMM 5.2+ format, directly loadable. Title block documents all generation assumptions.

Map PNG: City map only, or city map + network overlay. 2400×1600 px at 2× DPI.

DEM (ASCII Grid): ESRI ASCII raster (.asc) with IDW-interpolated elevation grid from junction elevations. Compatible with ArcGIS, QGIS, and any raster GIS tool.

Copy to Clipboard: Full .inp text copied for pasting into any text editor.

Visualization Controls

City Map: Toggle city backdrop on/off. Network: Toggle pipe/conduit overlay. Labels: Toggle district, park, lake, and city name labels. Elevation: Node elevation heatmap (blue=low, red=high). Voronoi: Thiessen polygon boundaries colored by demand/land-use zone.

Spatial Distribution Formulas

C-Factor / Pipe Age Pattern: Pipes are assigned roughness coefficients and installation years based on distance from the grid center. The formula uses normalized distance d = dist(node, center) / maxDist where d=0 is downtown (center) and d=1 is suburban (edge).

C-factor = Cmin + d * (Cmax - Cmin) + random_jitter
Downtown (center, d~0): C = 80-100 (old, rough pipes)
Suburban (edge, d~1): C = 110-130 (new, smooth pipes)
Install Year = 1950 + d * 70
Downtown: 1950s-1960s (oldest infrastructure)
Suburban: 2000s-2020s (newest infrastructure)

Demand Zones: Four zones are spatially seeded: Industrial (NW quadrant), Commercial (central spine), Mixed (defined area), Residential (fill). Each junction is assigned to the nearest zone seed point.

Pipe Diameters: Transmission mains (24-36") near reservoirs and along every 5th row/column. Distribution pipes (6-12") elsewhere. Diameter decreases with distance from sources.

Architecture

Single self-contained HTML file with no external dependencies. All generation logic, rendering, city map procedural generation, BFS algorithms, Hazen-Williams friction calculator, design storm hyetographs, and .inp file builders run entirely in the browser. Seeded PRNG (Mulberry32) ensures reproducible results for the same grid dimensions. Mobile-optimized with bottom drawer controls, 44px touch targets, and iOS zoom prevention.
