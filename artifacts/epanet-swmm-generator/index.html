EPANET 2.3 & SWMM 5.2.4 Network Generator

Single-file HTML application • ~160 KB • No dependencies • Mobile-optimized

Overview

This application generates hydraulically valid, calibration-ready water distribution (EPANET) and stormwater collection (SWMM) models of any size — from 50-node teaching examples to 100,000-node city-scale networks. Each model includes a procedurally generated city map backdrop with streets, buildings, parks, lakes, rivers, railroads, and highways. Models are exportable as .inp files that load directly into EPANET 2.2+, EPA SWMM 5.2+, WNTR, PySWMM, InfoWorks ICM, or any compatible hydraulic modeling platform.

EPANET 2.3 Mode — Water Distribution

Network Topology: Grid-based layout with configurable jitter (position perturbation), diagonal cross-connections for looping, and dead-end branches. Grid size from 3×3 to 316×316. Eight presets from Tiny (50 nodes) through City (100K nodes).

Terrain: Five terrain models — rolling hills with NW→SE slope, flat, central valley, ridge line, and random Perlin-like noise. Configurable elevation range (default 58–202 ft) with multiple hill features.

Reservoirs: Placed at perimeter nodes and fully integrated into the grid — the reservoir replaces the junction node, inheriting all pipe connections. No floating nodes. Source pumps are only created when the reservoir head is insufficient for gravity feed.

Pump Curves: Sized via BFS critical-path analysis. The engine traces from each reservoir through the pipe network using Hazen-Williams friction loss to find the farthest node, then computes the required pump head as elevation deficit + computed friction + safety margin. Three-point curves bracket the operating point (shutoff = 150% design head, max flow = 400% design flow or design+500 GPM whichever is larger) to prevent "exceeds maximum flow" warnings. Booster pumps are sized based on actual pipe diameter and downstream elevation needs.

Tanks: Placed in the distribution field. Base elevation computed to serve the local pressure zone at ~85% of source HGL. Tank nodes replace junction nodes, same as reservoirs.

Pipes: Length = Euclidean distance × 1.3 street-routing factor. Diameters: transmission (24–36") along corridors and near sources, secondary (12–20") on arterials every 5th row/column, distribution (6–12") elsewhere. C-factors spatially correlated: downtown (center) = old pipes (C=80–100), suburban (edges) = new pipes (C=110–130).

Pipe Age: Each pipe assigned an installation year correlated with distance from center (1950s downtown → 2020s suburban). Written as decade-tagged link attributes (YR1960s, YR1990s, etc.).

Pumps & Valves: No parallel links. Every pump and valve is inserted by splitting an existing pipe and creating an intermediate junction. Booster pumps include a closed bypass pipe for isolation. PRVs are placed at elevation zone boundaries (pressure zones auto-detected when terrain range exceeds 80 ft).

Pressure Zones: When the elevation range exceeds 80 ft, the system is divided into zones of ~70 ft each. PRVs are placed at pipes that cross zone boundaries, with settings computed from downstream elevation + 50 psi target.

Demand Zones: Four zones spatially clustered (not random): Industrial seeded in NW corner, Commercial along a central spine, Mixed in a defined area, Residential fills everywhere else. Four diurnal patterns with realistic multipliers (residential morning/evening peaks, commercial midday, industrial plateau, mixed blend).

Calibration Features: Emitter nodes (leaks/hydrants), optional chlorine water quality model with bulk/wall decay, secondary demands for unaccounted-for water and fire flow, node/pipe tags for zone-based grouping.

Connectivity Validation: Post-generation BFS from all sources verifies every junction is reachable. Isolated node count displayed in Stats.

Supply-Demand Check: Total average and peak demand (×1.6 peaking factor) are computed and compared to supply capacity. Green/red indicator in Stats tab.

SWMM 5.2.4 Mode — Stormwater & Sanitary Sewer

Junctions: Manholes with invert elevation = ground elevation − cover depth. Max depth computed from ground-to-invert difference. Spatially clustered land-use zones control impervious percentage.

Subcatchments: Drainage areas delineated using D8 flow direction routing on the DEM grid. Each cell flows to its steepest downhill neighbor until reaching a local pit or grid edge; all cells draining to the same terminus are aggregated into a subcatchment. Area, width, slope, and percent impervious vary by land-use zone.

Conduits: Gravity sewers oriented from higher to lower invert elevation. 10 conduit cross-section shapes: Circular, Rectangular Closed, Arch, Egg, Horseshoe, Gothic, Catenary, Basket Handle, Semi-Elliptical, Semi-Circular — with configurable mix percentages. Diameters grade from collectors (12–24") at periphery to trunk sewers (36–60") at center.

Culverts: Configurable count with entrance loss coefficients (Kentry=0.5, Kexit=1.0) and FHWA culvert codes (circular concrete, CMP, box concrete, box headwall).

Outfalls: Placed at the lowest-elevation perimeter nodes. FREE discharge type.

Design Storms: SCS Type I, SCS Type II (24-hr), Uniform (6-hr), and Chicago (2-hr) design storms with configurable total depth. Written as TIMESERIES at 30-min or 5-min intervals.

Infiltration: Three methods — Horton (with configurable max/min rates and decay constant), Green-Ampt (suction, Ksat, IMD), and Curve Number. Parameters vary by subcatchment.

Water Quality: Up to 8 pollutants (TSS, BOD, COD, TN, TP, Zinc, Oil & Grease, E.coli). EMC washoff with zone-specific concentrations. Four land uses (Residential, Commercial, Industrial, OpenSpace) with coverage assignments. Full POLLUTANTS, LANDUSES, COVERAGES, BUILDUP, and WASHOFF sections.

Groundwater: Aquifer definition with configurable porosity, field capacity, Ksat, and groundwater table elevation. Per-subcatchment GROUNDWATER entries with A1/B1 lateral flow coefficients.

RDII (Rainfall-Derived Inflow & Infiltration): Five spatially-distributed RTK unit hydrograph groups (UH1–UH5). Old downtown sewers get UH1 (high R values = more I&I), newer suburban sewers get UH5 (low R). RTK parameters use realistic statistical distributions rather than uniform random sampling. Configurable % of nodes receiving RDII and sewer area per node.

RDII Statistical Distribution Basis

R (Volume Fraction) — Weibull Degradation Curve: R values follow an age-dependent degradation model: R(age) = R_new + (R_max − R_new) × (1 − e^(−(age/τ)^n)), where R_new=0.01, R_max=0.12, τ=48 years, n=1.9. Calibrated anchor points: new pipes (~5 yr) R≈0.01, 20-year R≈0.03, 40-year R≈0.06, 60-year+ R≈0.10. Each UH group samples R from a log-normal distribution centered on the age-based value (σ=0.2–0.3). R2 ≈ 40% of R1 (interflow), R3 ≈ 15% of R1 (slow groundwater).

T (Time to Peak) — Area/Slope Correlation: T values scale with subcatchment area and real spatial average slope per zone: T_base × √(Area/10) × √(0.01/slope_zone). Flat zones (low slope) produce longer T (slower response); steep zones produce shorter T (faster runoff). Newer pipe groups have slightly longer T (less direct inflow). T1 (fast): 0.3–6.0 hr, T2 (medium): 1.0–24.0 hr, T3 (slow): 3.0–72.0 hr. Log-normal perturbation (σ=0.15–0.20) around the computed base.

K (Recession Ratio) — Log-Normal Distribution: K values are sampled from log-normal distributions: K1 ~ LogNormal(ln(2.5), 0.20), K2 ~ LogNormal(ln(3.0), 0.25), K3 ~ LogNormal(ln(3.5), 0.30). Longer responses have higher recession ratios, reflecting slower drainage from saturated soil.

Sewer Area — Beta Distribution: Contributing sewer area per node uses Beta(2,5) distribution scaled to the configured base area, producing a right-skewed distribution where most nodes have moderate areas with occasional larger contributors.

Based on empirical patterns from RDII calibration studies. See EPA SWMM Reference Manual Vol. I, Section 11.5 and Vallabhaneni et al. (2007) "SSOAP Toolbox".

Dry Weather Flow: Three zone-specific DWF patterns (Residential diurnal with morning/evening peaks, Commercial midday-heavy, Industrial sustained plateau). Optional weekend and monthly seasonal patterns. Configurable % of nodes with DWF and base flow magnitude.

Direct Inflows: Configurable number of nodes with triangle-hydrograph inflow timeseries. Staggered start times, configurable peak flow, ramp-up time, and duration. Optional TSS concentration timeseries with first-flush exponential decay.

LID Controls: Seven types — Bio-Retention Cell, Permeable Pavement, Infiltration Trench, Rain Barrel, Vegetative Swale, Green Roof, Rain Garden. Full multi-layer definitions (SURFACE, SOIL, STORAGE, PAVEMENT, DRAIN, DRAINMAT). Configurable % of subcatchments receiving LID.

Snowmelt: Optional. Dividing temperature, melt coefficient, ATI weight. Snowpack definitions for plowable, impervious, and pervious surfaces. Temperature timeseries with diurnal variation.

Spatial Distribution Formulas

C-Factor / Pipe Age Pattern: Pipes are assigned roughness coefficients and installation years based on distance from the grid center. The formula uses normalized distance d = dist(node, center) / maxDist where d=0 is downtown (center) and d=1 is suburban (edge).

C-factor = Cmin + d * (Cmax - Cmin) + random_jitter
Downtown (center, d~0): C = 80-100 (old, rough pipes)
Suburban (edge, d~1): C = 110-130 (new, smooth pipes)
Install Year = 1950 + d * 70
Downtown: 1950s-1960s (oldest infrastructure)
Suburban: 2000s-2020s (newest infrastructure)

Demand Zones: Four zones are spatially seeded: Industrial (NW quadrant), Commercial (central spine), Mixed (defined area), Residential (fill). Each junction is assigned to the nearest zone seed point.

Pipe Diameters: Transmission mains (24-36") near reservoirs and along every 5th row/column. Distribution pipes (6-12") elsewhere. Diameter decreases with distance from sources.

EPANET Fundamentals Reference

A comprehensive guide from first principles of hydraulics through professional practice standards. Based on EPA EPANET 2.2 documentation, William James's Rules for Responsible Modeling, and AWWA standards. Click any section to expand.

Part 1: Introduction to Hydraulics

A hydraulic model is a mathematical representation of a real water distribution system using conservation of mass and energy to simulate pressures, flows, velocities, and water quality at every point.

EPANET solves one fundamental problem at every timestep: Energy In = Energy Out + Energy Lost

The Two Conservation Laws:

For every node:  ΣQ_in = ΣQ_out + Demand   (Conservation of Mass)
For every loop:  Σh_L around loop = 0       (Conservation of Energy)

The Modeling Hierarchy:

Level 1: Sketch10-50 nodes, no calibration, 30min-2hr build
Level 2: Planning100-2,000 nodes, ±15% pressure/±20% flow, 1-4 weeks
Level 3: Operational2,000-50,000+ nodes, ±5% pressure/±10% flow, 2-6 months
Level 4: All-Pipes10,000-500,000+ nodes, comprehensive field testing, 6-18 months
Part 2: The Three Analysis Types

A. Steady-State Analysis

A single snapshot in time. All demands, tank levels, and pump statuses are fixed. EPANET finds the unique set of flows and pressures satisfying both conservation laws. Set DURATION 0:00 in [TIMES].

Use for: fire flow analysis, pipe break scenarios, new development impact, quick model validation.

B. Extended Period Simulation (EPS)

A sequence of steady-state solutions linked by time-varying demands, tank level changes, and pump control logic. Between each timestep, EPANET updates demand multipliers, evaluates controls, updates tank levels, and re-solves.

Why 72 hours, not 24? Day 1 is influenced by assumed initial conditions. Days 2-3 reach a repeating daily cycle representing true operational behavior. A well-calibrated EPS shows tank levels returning to approximately the same level after 24 hours.

C. Transient Analysis (Water Hammer)

EPANET does NOT model transients. Rapid pressure waves from sudden valve closure or pump trip require dedicated software (Bentley HAMMER, InfoSurge, AFT Impulse). Per AWWA M51, transient analysis is mandatory when pump station >100 HP, pipe diameter >24", pipeline >1 mile without surge protection, or valve closure <10 seconds.

Joukowsky equation: ΔP = ρ × a × ΔV / g. Example: V changes 5→0 ft/s with wave speed 4000 ft/s → 269 psi surge on top of normal 60 psi = 329 psi peak.

Part 3: Why Hydraulic Theory Matters

The Black Box Trap

Conservation of Mass ignored"System Unbalanced" error
Conservation of Energy ignoredNegative pressures at hilltop nodes
Wrong friction formula/C-factorsWildly wrong pressures everywhere
Pump Affinity Laws violated"Pump exceeds maximum flow" warning
Tank mass balance wrongTanks drain empty or overflow forever
Minor losses at fittings5-15% headloss error in heavily-fitted runs

Hierarchy of Errors (highest impact first):

  1. Topology — disconnected nodes, wrong connections → entire zones show zero flow
  2. Demands — wrong magnitude/spatial allocation → system-wide pressure bias
  3. Elevations — wrong datum/unit conversion → individual nodes show impossible pressures
  4. Roughness — wrong C-factors → gradient of pressure drop along pipe runs
  5. Control Logic — wrong tank triggers/pump schedules → EPS cycling doesn't match SCADA
  6. Minor Losses — missing K-factors → small (<5%) local headloss errors

Nodes (Points Where Things Happen)

Part 5: The Mathematics — Global Gradient Algorithm

EPANET uses Todini and Pilati's (1988) Global Gradient Algorithm (GGA) — a Newton-Raphson variant that simultaneously solves all unknown heads and flows.

Core Equations:

Pipe headloss:    h_L(ij) = r × Q(ij) × |Q(ij)|^(n-1) + m × Q(ij) × |Q(ij)|
  r = resistance coefficient, n = flow exponent (1.852 H-W, 2.0 D-W)

Energy conservation:  H(i) - H(j) = h_L(ij)
Mass conservation:    Σ Q(ij) - D(i) = 0

Solved iteratively: |ΔH| < ACCURACY AND |ΔQ| < ACCURACY

Convergence Behavior:

Healthy model6 iterations: 45.2 → 12.8 → 3.1 → 0.4 → 0.02 → 0.0008 ft → CONVERGED
Slow convergence40 iterations, still 12.3 ft → "System Unbalanced" warning
Divergence45.2 → 89.4 → 500+ ft → "Equations cannot be solved" error

Controls: ACCURACY 0.001 (default), TRIALS 40, UNBALANCED CONTINUE 10

Allocation Methods:

  1. Manual — count connections per node (<100 nodes)
  2. Proportional by pipe length — distribute zone demand by half-length of connected pipes
  3. Thiessen polygons — GIS-based Voronoi areas, sum meter demands

Part 9: Pumps and Energy

Critical: EPANET needs at least 3 points for a proper pump curve — shutoff head (zero flow), design point (near BEP), and maximum flow.

[CURVES]
;ID      Flow(GPM)  Head(ft)
PUMP1    0          180     ; Shutoff head
PUMP1    500        150     ; Design point (near BEP)
PUMP1    1000       100     ; Maximum flow

Pump Affinity Laws (VFD speed changes):

Q₂/Q₁ = N₂/N₁         (flow ∝ speed)
H₂/H₁ = (N₂/N₁)²     (head ∝ speed²)
P₂/P₁ = (N₂/N₁)³     (power ∝ speed³)

At 85% speed: Flow=85%, Head=72.25%, Power=61.4% → huge savings!

Energy Cost Calculation:

Power (kW) = Q × H × SG / (3960 × η)
  Q=800 GPM, H=120 ft, η=0.75 → 32.3 kW
  18 hr/day at $0.10/kWh → $58.14/day
  + demand charge 32.3 kW × $50/kW = $1,615/mo

Pump Controls:

Simple: LINK PUMP1 OPEN IF NODE TANK1 BELOW 20.0. Rule-based: IF/AND/OR/THEN with priority. Rules override simple controls; last matching rule wins.

Part 10: Valves and Pressure Management

PRV — Three Operating Modes:

ACTIVEUpstream P > Setting — valve throttles to maintain downstream P = setting
FULLY OPENUpstream can't reach setting even wide open — acts like a pipe
CLOSEDDownstream P > Upstream P — prevents reverse flow (like check valve)

Other Valve Types:

PSV — maintains upstream pressure at minimum value. FCV — limits flow to set rate (only limits, not guarantees). PBV — fixed pressure drop. TCV — simulates partially closed valve. GPV — custom headloss vs flow curve.

Pressure Zone Design Rule:

If elevation range >150 ft, multiple pressure zones needed. Rule of thumb: one zone per ~100-150 ft of elevation range. Target: 40-80 psi throughout each zone.

Part 11: Water Quality Modeling

Three WQ Simulation Modes:

  1. Chemical concentration — track chlorine decay, DBP growth, or conservative tracers
  2. Water age — hours since water entered from any source (identifies stagnation)
  3. Source tracing — % of water at each node from a specific source

Chlorine Decay Model:

Bulk decay: dC/dt = -Kb × C^n  (in water column)
Wall decay: Rate = Kw × C       (at pipe wall)

Typical values:
  Kb (bulk):  -0.5 to -1.5 /day
  Kw (wall):  -0.5 to -2.0 ft/day
  Source:     1.5 to 2.5 mg/L
  Target:     >0.2 mg/L (EPA minimum)

Water Age Standards:

<24 hoursGood — no action needed
24-48 hoursMonitor chlorine residual
48-72 hoursConsider flushing program
>72 hoursActive intervention: flushing, looping, blow-offs, booster chlorination
Part 12: Building Your First EPANET Model

Step-by-Step Workflow:

  1. Define Purpose & Scope — what decision will this model inform? What level of accuracy is needed?
  2. Gather Data — pipe inventory, elevations (DEM/LiDAR), demands (billing), tank dimensions, pump curves, valve locations, SCADA data
  3. Build Skeleton — reservoirs → tanks → transmission mains → pumps → distribution grid → smaller pipes → assign elevations → assign diameters/roughness
  4. Assign Demands — total from production records, allocate to nodes, add UAW (15-25%), assign patterns
  5. Set Controls & Options — units, headloss formula, timesteps, 72-hour EPS duration
  6. Run & Debug — check for System Unbalanced, negative pressures, pump errors
  7. Calibrate — compare to field measurements, adjust: demands → roughness → valves → pumps
  8. Analyze & Report — run scenarios, document assumptions, communicate uncertainty

Level 3: Comprehensive (Ops)±1 psi±5%±0.5 ft

Adjustment Priority (highest impact first):

  1. Demands — total matches production? UAW added? Spatial distribution reasonable? Patterns match SCADA?
  2. Roughness / C-factors — appropriate for pipe material & age? Old unlined CI: C=60-90. New PVC: C=140-150
  3. Valve settings — PRV settings field-verified? Closed/partially-closed valves documented? (#1 hidden error)
  4. Pump curves — from recent field test (not nameplate)? Worn impellers reduce head 10-20%
  5. Tank operations — trigger levels match SCADA setpoints?

The Critical Rule:

"Calibration without validation is self-deception." — William James

Calibrate with Dataset A, then validate with Dataset B (NO further adjustments). If validation fails → model is overfit to Dataset A, not the physics.

Part 15: Common Mistakes and Troubleshooting

Error: "System Unbalanced"

  1. Disconnected nodes (most common) — junction has no open pipe path to any source. Run connectivity check.
  2. Impossible demands — total demand exceeds supply capacity. Verify peak × base is realistic.
  3. Pump curve errors — shutoff head lower than highest node. Provide 3-point curve.
  4. PRV/PSV conflicts — two PRVs in series with conflicting settings, or PSV downstream of PRV.
  5. Tight convergence — relax ACCURACY to 0.01, increase TRIALS to 100, set UNBALANCED CONTINUE 10.

Error: Negative Pressures

  1. Check elevation units (ft entered as m? km as m?)
  2. Check source head — reservoir head high enough? Pump provides enough head?
  3. Check connectivity — open hydraulic path from source to node?
  4. Check demand magnitude — unit error? (m³/hr in GPM field?)
  5. Consider PDA mode: DEMAND MODEL PDA, MINIMUM PRESSURE 0, REQUIRED PRESSURE 25

Error: "Pump Cannot Deliver Flow/Head"

System demands more flow than curve allows, or system head exceeds shutoff. Fix: add 3 curve points, increase speed, add pumps in series (head) or parallel (flow), reduce downstream demand, or increase pipe sizes.

Part 4: Calibration Practice Exercises

Exercise 1: Manual Roughness Calibration (Beginner)

  1. Generate Medium model (750 nodes), save as "truth_model.inp"
  2. Run in EPANET/WNTR, record pressures at 20 random nodes (your "field measurements")
  3. Copy to "calibration_model.inp", change ALL C-factors to 120 (destroy spatial variation)
  4. Adjust C-factors until pressures match within ±3 psi — no peeking at truth model!
Python (WNTR):
  wn_truth = wntr.network.WaterNetworkModel('truth_model.inp')
  results = wntr.sim.EpanetSimulator(wn_truth).run_sim()
  # Record "field" pressures at 20 random nodes at hour 12
  # Then corrupt, calibrate, compute RMSE

Exercise 2: Automated Calibration with Optimization (Intermediate)

  1. Group pipes by diameter: Large (>12"), Medium (8-12"), Small (<8")
  2. Define objective function: RMSE between model and "field" pressures
  3. Use scipy.optimize.differential_evolution with bounds [60, 150] for C-factors
  4. Optimizer finds optimal C-factor per group automatically

Exercise 3: Demand Pattern Calibration (Advanced)

  1. Compare model total system flow to "observed" SCADA hourly data (24 values)
  2. Scale pattern multipliers: target = observed_flow / (total_base_demand × conversion)
  3. Apply calibrated multipliers to pattern, re-run, verify flow match
  4. Plot model vs SCADA overlay to visualize calibration quality

EPANET Model Rules & Data Reference

Complete data model, object properties, controls, water quality, analysis options, hydraulic reference tables, fire flow analysis, troubleshooting tips, and error codes — extracted from the EPANET-UI Manual (OWA).

Data Model & Object Properties

Network Structure:

EPANET models a pipe network as links connected to nodes. Nodes: Junctions (demands), Tanks (variable level), Reservoirs (fixed head). Links: Pipes (length/diameter/roughness), Pumps (curve or constant power), Valves (flow or pressure regulation).

Supporting objects: Time Patterns, Data Curves, Simple Controls, Rule-Based Controls, Water Quality Sources.

Junction Properties:

Junction IDUnique label, up to 31 characters
Elevationft or m above common reference. Used to compute pressure from hydraulic head.
Base DemandAverage demand in flow units. Negative = external source of flow into junction.
Demand PatternTime pattern ID providing multipliers applied to Base Demand over time.
Demand CategoriesMultiple base demands and patterns for different user categories at one junction.
Emitter CoefficientDischarge coefficient for emitter/sprinkler. Flow units at 1 unit of pressure drop. 0 = no emitter.
TagOptional text (no spaces) for categorization (e.g., pressure zone).

Reservoir Properties:

ElevationFixed water surface elevation (ft or m). Defines hydraulic head boundary.
Elevation PatternTime pattern for time-varying reservoir elevation (e.g., tie-in to another system).

Tank Properties:

ElevationTank bottom elevation (not ground elevation)
Initial/Min/Max LevelHeight above bottom at start, minimum floor, maximum cap
Diameterft or m. Square/rect: equivalent diameter = 1.128 × √(area)
Volume CurveVolume vs. water level. If none, tank is assumed cylindrical.
Can OverflowYES = overflow/spillage. Otherwise inflow links temporarily closed when full.
Mixing ModelMIXED (complete), 2COMP (two-compartment), FIFO (plug flow), LIFO

Pipe Properties:

Length / DiameterActual length (ft/m), diameter (inches/mm)
RoughnessUnitless (H-W), millifeet/mm (D-W), s/m^(1/3) (C-M)
Loss CoefficientMinor loss coefficient for bends, fittings (unitless)
Initial StatusOPEN, CLOSED, or CV (check valve — flow from Start to End only)
Leak AreaAvg leak opening (sq mm) × leaks per 100 units of pipe length
Leak ExpansionRate of expansion (sq mm/pressure head) × leaks per 100 units. 0.1-0.001 plastic, 0 iron.

Pump Properties:

Pump CurveHead delivered vs. flow rate. Not needed for constant energy pumps.
PowerConstant power (hp/kW). Ignored if pump curve assigned.
SpeedRelative speed (1.0 = normal). Speed Pattern controls time variation.
Efficiency CurveWire-to-water efficiency (%) vs. flow rate. For energy calculation only.

Valve Properties & Placement Rules:

TypesPRV, PSV, PBV (pressure), FCV (flow), TCV (throttle), GPV (general purpose)
SettingPressure (PRV/PSV/PBV), flow (FCV), loss coeff (TCV), head loss curve ID (GPV)
Initial StatusOPEN, CLOSED, or ACTIVE (normal operating mode)

Placement rules: PRV/PSV cannot connect directly to reservoir/tank (use intermediate junction). PRVs/PSVs cannot share same downstream node or be in series. Two PSVs cannot share same upstream node. FCVs cannot connect directly to reservoir/tank. GPV cannot connect to another GPV.

Controls: Simple & Rule-Based

Simple Controls:

LINK linkID action IF NODE nodeID ABOVE/BELOW value
LINK linkID action AT TIME time
LINK linkID action AT CLOCKTIME clocktime

Actions: OPEN, CLOSED, pump speed, valve setting
Pipes with CVs cannot have controls.

Warning: Paired pressure controls to open/close a link can cause instability if thresholds are too close. Use rule-based controls instead.

Rule-Based Controls:

RULE ruleID
IF   condition_1
AND  condition_2
OR   condition_3
THEN action_1
AND  action_2
ELSE action_3
PRIORITY value

Objects:  NODE, LINK, SYSTEM, JUNCTION, PIPE, PUMP, TANK, VALVE
Node attrs: DEMAND, HEAD, PRESSURE
Tank attrs: LEVEL, FILLTIME, DRAINTIME
Link attrs: FLOW, STATUS, SETTING
System:   DEMAND, TIME, CLOCKTIME
Operators: =, IS, <>, NOT, <, BELOW, >, ABOVE, <=, >=

Precedence: OR has HIGHER precedence than AND
  IF A or B and C → (A or B) and C

Example — Tank-Level Pump Control:

RULE 1
IF TANK 1 LEVEL ABOVE 19.1
THEN PUMP 335 STATUS IS CLOSED
AND  PIPE 330 STATUS IS OPEN

RULE 2
IF TANK 1 LEVEL BELOW 17.1
THEN PUMP 335 STATUS IS OPEN
AND  PIPE 330 STATUS IS CLOSED

PRIORITY: Higher value overrides lower. Rules without priority always lose to rules with priority. Equal priorities: first listed wins.

Fire Flow Analysis

For each selected node, determines: (1) pressure when target fire flow is added to normal demand, (2) largest fire flow maintaining pressures above target.

Setup Parameters:

  • Target fire flow value and target pressure to maintain
  • Time of day (for extended period simulations)
  • Node selection: individual, by tag, by region, or all
  • Pressure zone scope: individual node, all selected, or all network

Analysis Method:

  1. Run with no fire flow → if static pressure < target, available fire flow = 0
  2. Add full target fire flow → if target pressure met at all zone nodes, available = full target
  3. Otherwise, Ridder's Method finds largest fire flow meeting target pressure
Troubleshooting & Modeling Tips
Sizing pump for flowSet CLOSED. Add +demand at suction, −demand at discharge. Head difference = required pump head.
Sizing pump for headReplace pump with reverse PBV. Design head → PBV pressure setting. Flow through = design flow.
Groundwater wellReservoir (head = aquifer piezometric head) + pump. Or junction with negative demand = pumping rate.
Source flow scheduleReplace reservoirs with junctions having negative demands = scheduled flows. Keep ≥1 tank/reservoir.
Fire flow at junctionPressure check: add fire flow to demand. Available flow: set emitter coeff large, add 2.3×psi to elevation.
Backflow preventerGPV with headloss curve (increasing loss with decreasing flow) + CV pipe in series.
Pneumatic tankShort wide cylinder, elevation ≈ pressure head rating. Area = (V₂−V₁)/(y−x).
WQ initial conditionsAssign measured values at monitored nodes, interpolate elsewhere. Tank age ≈ 24/(fraction exchanged daily).
THM growth1st-order saturation: bulk coeff = +0.7/(THM doubling time), limiting conc = max THM level.
Chlorine boosterSource Type = SETPOINT BOOSTER or FLOW PACED BOOSTER at junction/tank node.

16.4 An Eyewitness Account — The Workshop That Started It All

By the late 1980s, the water distribution modeling world had made impressive strides in hydraulic simulation — steady-state solvers, extended-period simulation, calibration methods — but water quality modeling in pipe networks was still in its infancy. A handful of researchers were working on the problem, but there was no consensus tool, no standard approach, and no coordination between the groups.

That changed on February 4–5, 1991, when the AWWA Research Foundation and the U.S. EPA co-sponsored a workshop titled "Water Quality Modeling in Distribution Systems" at EPA's Andrew W. Breidenbach Environmental Research Center in Cincinnati. Forty-three of the top water distribution modelers in the world were in that room — Uri Shamir, Walter Grayman, Don Wood, Robert Clark, and many others. The purpose was to assess the state of the art, identify gaps, and chart a path forward for water quality modeling in drinking water networks.

I was one of the 43 participants. What I remember most vividly is someone who was not on the official program. Lew Rossman, an EPA researcher known at the time primarily for his work on rainfall-runoff modeling (what would eventually become SWMM5), sat quietly through both days of presentations and discussions. He took notes. He asked a few questions. He did not present a paper or give a talk.

Then, shortly after the workshop ended, Lew walked up to Bob Clark — who headed EPA's drinking water research program — and essentially said: "I can build that model." Bob gave him the green light, and within roughly two years Lew had produced EPANET — a hydraulic and water quality model for distribution systems that was more capable, better documented, and more accessible than anything the 43 experts in that room had envisioned.

What made EPANET remarkable was not just the technical achievement but the decisions around it: public domain release, clear documentation with worked examples, and a modular codebase others could read and extend. Those decisions created the entire commercial and academic ecosystem we know today — WaterGEMS, InfoWater, WaterCAD, and dozens of other tools all trace their lineage back to that moment.

Years later, Lew turned his attention back to the stormwater side and undertook a complete rewrite of SWMM — producing SWMM5 (released in 2004), which replaced the aging Fortran codebase with clean, modular C. The fact that one person created the definitive models for both drinking water distribution and urban stormwater drainage is, as far as I know, without parallel in the water engineering world.

More than thirty years later, the tools Lew built in that Cincinnati office remain the engines beneath virtually every water distribution and urban drainage model in use worldwide. It all started with a quiet man sitting in the back of a workshop, listening carefully, and then volunteering to build what everyone else was only talking about.

— Tom Walski, Ph.D., P.E.
Industry Advisor, Bentley Systems
